To the little dreamer with a heart of gold,

Everything happens for a reason. You will come to understand this and trust the process. It’ll take some time with quite a few obstacles along the way. However, even in your lowest moments where you feel like giving up, you’ll see the light and find the clarity to persevere. From the moment that you open your eyes, take your first steps, utter your first words… You’ll enter an ever-changing journey more complex and bigger than your wildest dreams.

And you have quite a lot of those. Dreams so big and intricate that one could say you’ve seen the creation of your own universe from countless perspective, brought about by many different life changing experiences that you’ll have. Some good, some bad… But all of them carry wisdom that you’ll find within yourself to harness, so that the next time you have to affront another trial, you’re better than before. The places you’ll go, the people you’ll meet, the things you’ll see. They will all touch your heart, but you will never let it change your core.

They will imprint lessons in you that will ignite your soul with an everlasting fire, so warm that at times you’ll feel like you cannot control your emotions, and you will find yourself bursting with passion to fight for those dreams that you know in your heart that you want to make true. To this I say, don’t ever lose that passion. You will learn to control it, you will understand when to use it, and you will manifest into your life every single thing you set out to accomplish when you do your absolute best and stay true to yourself.

Don’t ever let anyone make you stray from who you are. There will be many that will attempt to influence you into silencing your voice, into forgetting where your heart is, and into giving up your soul for their own gain. You will learn the hard way what to do, and how to fight back. But you will cross that bridge, you will become stronger than you ever were before, and you will find so much love within yourself to conquer anything in your way. Reclaim your shine and be the brightest star in your universe.

Don’t stop believing, don’t stop dreaming, and don’t ever stop loving.

— Yours truly.

Hello there muses, happy Tuesday!

When talking about art in any way, shape or form a lot of male artists can come easily to mind, but not so many female ones, right? That’s why my article for today is about female artists forgotten by history, so without further ado, let’s get to it.

10. Hannah Höch (1889 – 1978)

She was a German Dada artist, and one of the first to do photomontage, which consisted of pasted photographs pulled from the press and such.

Höch introduced the concept of “New Woman”, aka, an energetic, professional and androgynous woman, who is ready to take their place as man’s equal.

Another of her interests was social roles and who is organizing them, as well.

9. Lyubov Popova (1889–1924)

One of the most talented and influential artists of the avant-garde Russia.

Popova, who tried herself as Cubist, Suprematist and Constructivist, developed in 1915 her own variant of non-objective art.

In 1921 she turned completely to industrial design, where she excelled at designing clothing and fabrics and produced posters, book designs, ceramics, and photomontages.

8. Hilma af Klint (1862 – 1944)

Born and raised in Sweden, af Klint, an artist and a mystic, was a pioneer at abstract art; her paintings were amongst the first ones of that style.

She belonged to a group of women called “The Five”, who were the creators of experimental automatic drawing as early as 1896.

7. Margaret Macdonald Mackintosh (1864–1933)

She was a Scottish artist whose work became part of the defining features of what we know as “Glasgow Style”, during the 1890’s.

Along with her sister, Frances, they were students at the Glasgow School of Art, both of them studying design.

Mackintosh was able to work with a variety of things, such as metalwork, embroidery, and textile. She would later collaborate with her husband, the architect and designer Charles Rennie Mackintosh.

Her inspiration came from Celtic imagery, poems by Morris and Rossetti, literature, symbolism, and folklore.

6. Harriet Powers (1837–1910)

Powers was an African-American freed slave and a folk artist who created quilts.

The techniques she used were traditional apliqué ones, which allowed her to record things such as local legends, Bible stories, and astronomical events.

Both quilts depicted above are the only ones of all her work known to survive.

A letter was discovered in 2009, which allowed us to learn that Harriet was a literate woman who would read well-known stories and depict them in her beautiful quilts.

Source: DailyArt Magazine.
